John Coltrane

Known for:

Drug-addict turned spiritual artist.
Writing "the most feared song in jazz".
Only horn player to match Miles Davis in their time.

Chosen Quote:

"My music is the spiritual expression of what I am. (I want) to help humanity free itself from its hangups.
I want to speak to their souls."

This villainous man in the photograph, this hideous figure from my childhood, is Kenny G .

He plays smooth jazz of the simplest, corniest, most repetitive kind.

My mom played his cd's over and over. I was haunted by the same song structures, same chord progressions, choruses (in songs without vocals) corny enough to make Mariah Carey gag (in Christmas) ... It was hell.

So in my childhood , I thought hell was jazz . Kenny G was Satan.

But every now and then I would get glimpses on TV, on magazine covers, on album covers, of what real Jazz looked like: Duke Ellington, John Coltrane, Ella Fitzgerald...

Mostly black people who didn't have their own helicopters like Kenny G, But whose elegance and mystique went beyond money , and their music was beyond complex.

Like Baudelaire's cursed poets, these musicians were not after record sales or recognition, but after the creative freedom of Bebop Jazz , and their relentless pursuit fascinated me.

Bebop Jazz is hell-bent on breaking the musical status quo. It can be as difficult to understand as contemporary dance.

This, unfortunately, means that Bebop Jazz has a very reduced, savvy audience.

In my fascination and desperation trying to understand this mystical musical genre, I got my hands on the Jazz documentary by Ken Burns.

That docuseries attempts to demistify, contextualize and help appreciate jazz for non-musically-trained listeners. It was there that I found him. The Giant. The Cool Train. The shaman of jazz.

They were the Ali vs Foreman of Jazz in the 50s and 60s.

In my eyes, Miles Davis was the wife beater, the man who shunned reporters and disrespected fans, even though his reputation as a musician had been damaged by heroin use.

Davis even had his voice damaged for the rest of his life due to his temper.

He had vocal chord surgery. He was told he wasn't allowed to scream or yell at people for a time. His temper got the better of him during post-op.

Little did I know that Davis had actually fired Coltrane from his quintet due to his insistent drug and alcohol abuse. Apparently, given Davis's own experience, he had been quite tolerant and patient, giving Coltrane several chances to quit the habits.

But Coltrane didn't give them up, and Davis firing him was a rock bottom moment that changed him.

Coltrane quit cold turkey after that, relying on his spiritual beliefs, and his newly found work with Thelonious Monk, to survive the brutal withdrawal symptoms.

After hitting rock bottom, something pampered artists who own helicopters don't do much, Coltrane surrendered himself to his music being the best it could be and the most spiritual it could be.

Giant Steps has one of the most difficult chord progressions widely played in jazz, which became known as the Coltrane changes. This video explains their greatness in layman's terms.

A Love Supreme is not about romantic love. It is about Coltrane thanking God for providing him the strength to overcome his addictions.

All in all, I'm proud Coltrane lived his life as if he had watched every South Park episode with Kenny G in it .
